# Problem Set 05 – Detailed Solutions  
Electromagnetism I: Field and Forces

# Task 1 – Potential and Energy

## Problem Statement

For a point charge

$$
q = 4\,\mu\mathrm{C}
$$

calculate:

1. The electric potential at

$$
r = 0.3\,\mathrm{m}
$$

2. The potential difference between

$$
r_1 = 0.3\,\mathrm{m}
$$

and

$$
r_2 = 0.6\,\mathrm{m}
$$

3. The work required to move a test charge

$$
q_0 = 2\,\mu\mathrm{C}
$$

4. The electric field intensity obtained from the derivative of the potential.
5. Comparison with Coulomb’s law.

---

## Theory

The electric potential generated by a point charge is

$$
V(r) = k \frac{q}{r}
$$

where

$$
k = \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0}
$$

and numerically

$$
k \approx 9 \times 10^9\,\mathrm{N\,m^2/C^2}
$$

The electric field is related to the potential through

$$
\vec E = -\nabla V
$$

For radial symmetry:

$$
E(r) = -\frac{dV}{dr}
$$

The work required to move a charge through a potential difference is

$$
W = q_0 \Delta V
$$

---

## Step-by-Step Solution

### 1. Electric Potential at $r = 0.3\,\mathrm{m}$

The formula for the electric potential is

$$
V = k \frac{q}{r}
$$

Substitute the values:

$$
V = 9 \times 10^9 \cdot \frac{4 \times 10^{-6}}{0.3}
$$

First calculate the numerator:

$$
9 \times 10^9 \times 4 \times 10^{-6}
=
36 \times 10^3
=
36000
$$

Then divide by $0.3$:

$$
V = \frac{36000}{0.3}
$$

$$
V = 120000\,\mathrm{V}
$$

Therefore,

$$
V(0.3\,\mathrm{m}) = 1.2 \times 10^5\,\mathrm{V}
$$

---

### 2. Potential Difference

The potential difference is

$$
\Delta V = V(r_1) - V(r_2)
$$

First calculate the potential at

$$
r_2 = 0.6\,\mathrm{m}
$$

$$
V(0.6) =
9 \times 10^9
\cdot
\frac{4 \times 10^{-6}}{0.6}
$$

Again:

$$
9 \times 10^9 \times 4 \times 10^{-6}
=
36000
$$

Thus,

$$
V(0.6) = \frac{36000}{0.6}
$$

$$
V(0.6) = 60000\,\mathrm{V}
$$

Now compute the difference:

$$
\Delta V =
120000 - 60000
$$

$$
\Delta V = 60000\,\mathrm{V}
$$

---

### 3. Work Done on the Test Charge

The work required is

$$
W = q_0 \Delta V
$$

Substitute the values:

$$
W =
2 \times 10^{-6}
\cdot
60000
$$

$$
W = 0.12\,\mathrm{J}
$$

---

### 4. Electric Field from the Potential

The potential is

$$
V(r) = k \frac{q}{r}
$$

Differentiate with respect to $r$:

$$
\frac{dV}{dr}
=
-\frac{kq}{r^2}
$$

Therefore,

$$
E(r) = -\frac{dV}{dr}
$$

which gives

$$
E(r) = k \frac{q}{r^2}
$$

Now calculate the field at

$$
r = 0.3\,\mathrm{m}
$$

$$
E =
9 \times 10^9
\cdot
\frac{4 \times 10^{-6}}{(0.3)^2}
$$

Compute the denominator:

$$
(0.3)^2 = 0.09
$$

Thus,

$$
E =
\frac{36000}{0.09}
$$

$$
E = 4 \times 10^5\,\mathrm{N/C}
$$

---

### 5. Comparison with Coulomb’s Law

Coulomb’s law gives

$$
E = k \frac{q}{r^2}
$$

which is identical to the expression obtained from the derivative of the potential.

---

## Final Result

$$
V(0.3\,\mathrm{m}) = 1.2 \times 10^5\,\mathrm{V}
$$

$$
\Delta V = 6 \times 10^4\,\mathrm{V}
$$

$$
W = 0.12\,\mathrm{J}
$$

$$
E(0.3\,\mathrm{m}) = 4 \times 10^5\,\mathrm{N/C}
$$

---

## Interpretation

The electric potential decreases proportionally to $1/r$, while the electric field decreases faster, proportionally to $1/r^2$.

The electric field can therefore be interpreted as the spatial rate of change of the potential.

# Task 2 – Coulomb's Force

## Problem Statement

Two point charges are given:

$$
q_1 = 3\,\mu\mathrm{C}
$$

$$
q_2 = -5\,\mu\mathrm{C}
$$

located at:

$$
r_1 = (0,0)
$$

$$
r_2 = (0.4,0.3)\,\mathrm{m}
$$

Determine:

1. The force vector acting on $q_2$
2. The magnitude of the force
3. The potential energy of the system
4. The work required to separate the charges to a distance of $2\,\mathrm{m}$

---

## Theory

The Coulomb force vector is

$$
\vec F =
k \frac{q_1 q_2}{r^3}\vec r
$$

The electrostatic potential energy is

$$
U = k \frac{q_1 q_2}{r}
$$

---

## Step-by-Step Solution

### 1. Distance Between Charges

The displacement vector is

$$
\vec r = (0.4,0.3)
$$

The distance is

$$
r = \sqrt{0.4^2 + 0.3^2}
$$

$$
r = \sqrt{0.16 + 0.09}
$$

$$
r = \sqrt{0.25}
$$

$$
r = 0.5\,\mathrm{m}
$$

---

### 2. Magnitude of the Force

Use Coulomb’s law:

$$
F =
9 \times 10^9
\cdot
\frac{
(3 \times 10^{-6})
(5 \times 10^{-6})
}{
(0.5)^2
}
$$

Compute the product of charges:

$$
3 \times 10^{-6}
\cdot
5 \times 10^{-6}
=
15 \times 10^{-12}
$$

Thus,

$$
F =
9 \times 10^9
\cdot
\frac{15 \times 10^{-12}}{0.25}
$$

$$
F =
9 \times 10^9
\cdot
60 \times 10^{-12}
$$

$$
F = 0.54\,\mathrm{N}
$$

Since the charges have opposite signs, the force is attractive.

---

### 3. Force Vector

The unit vector is

$$
\hat r =
\left(
\frac{0.4}{0.5},
\frac{0.3}{0.5}
\right)
=
(0.8,0.6)
$$

The force vector acting on $q_2$ is directed toward $q_1$:

$$
\vec F = -0.54(0.8,0.6)
$$

Therefore,

$$
\vec F = (-0.432,-0.324)\,\mathrm{N}
$$

---

### 4. Potential Energy

Initial energy:

$$
U_i =
9 \times 10^9
\cdot
\frac{
(3 \times 10^{-6})
(-5 \times 10^{-6})
}{0.5}
$$

$$
U_i =
9 \times 10^9
\cdot
\frac{-15 \times 10^{-12}}{0.5}
$$

$$
U_i = -0.27\,\mathrm{J}
$$

---

### 5. Final Energy at $r = 2\,\mathrm{m}$

$$
U_f =
9 \times 10^9
\cdot
\frac{-15 \times 10^{-12}}{2}
$$

$$
U_f = -0.0675\,\mathrm{J}
$$

---

### 6. Work Required

The external work required is

$$
W = U_f - U_i
$$

$$
W = -0.0675 - (-0.27)
$$

$$
W = 0.2025\,\mathrm{J}
$$

---

## Final Result

$$
\vec F = (-0.432,-0.324)\,\mathrm{N}
$$

$$
|\vec F| = 0.54\,\mathrm{N}
$$

$$
U_i = -0.27\,\mathrm{J}
$$

$$
W = 0.2025\,\mathrm{J}
$$

---

## Interpretation

Because the charges have opposite signs, the interaction is attractive.

The negative potential energy indicates a bound electrostatic system.

Positive external work is required to separate the charges because work must be done against the attractive force.

# Task 3 – Electric Field from a System of Charges

## Problem Statement

Two point charges are given:

- Charge $+q$ located at

$$
(-a,0)
$$

- Charge $+2q$ located at

$$
(a,0)
$$

Determine:

1. The electric field vector

$$
\vec E(x,y)
$$

2. The conditions for

$$
E_x = 0
$$

and

$$
E_y = 0
$$

3. The field for:

$$
a = 0.2\,\mathrm{m}
$$

$$
y = 0.3\,\mathrm{m}
$$

$$
q = 2\,\mu\mathrm{C}
$$

4. The limit for

$$
y \gg a
$$

5. Whether a zero-field point exists on the $y$-axis.

---

## Theory

The electric field produced by a point charge is

$$
\vec E =
k \frac{q}{r^3}\vec r
$$

The total field is obtained from the superposition principle:

$$
\vec E = \vec E_1 + \vec E_2
$$

---

## Step-by-Step Solution

### 1. Position Vectors

For an arbitrary point $(x,y)$:

The vector from charge $q$ is

$$
\vec r_1 = (x+a,y)
$$

Its magnitude is

$$
r_1 = \sqrt{(x+a)^2 + y^2}
$$

The vector from charge $2q$ is

$$
\vec r_2 = (x-a,y)
$$

Its magnitude is

$$
r_2 = \sqrt{(x-a)^2 + y^2}
$$

---

### 2. Electric Field Components

Field from the first charge:

$$
\vec E_1 =
k \frac{q}{r_1^3}(x+a,y)
$$

Field from the second charge:

$$
\vec E_2 =
k \frac{2q}{r_2^3}(x-a,y)
$$

Therefore,

$$
\vec E =
k
\left[
\frac{q(x+a,y)}{r_1^3}
+
\frac{2q(x-a,y)}{r_2^3}
\right]
$$

---

### 3. Components Along the $y$-Axis

On the $y$-axis:

$$
x = 0
$$

Thus:

$$
r_1 = r_2 = \sqrt{a^2 + y^2}
$$

The $x$-component becomes

$$
E_x =
kq
\left(
\frac{a}{(a^2+y^2)^{3/2}}
-
\frac{2a}{(a^2+y^2)^{3/2}}
\right)
$$

$$
E_x =
-kq
\frac{a}{(a^2+y^2)^{3/2}}
$$

The $y$-component becomes

$$
E_y =
kq
\left(
\frac{y}{(a^2+y^2)^{3/2}}
+
\frac{2y}{(a^2+y^2)^{3/2}}
\right)
$$

$$
E_y =
3kq
\frac{y}{(a^2+y^2)^{3/2}}
$$

---

### 4. Numerical Calculation

Given:

$$
a = 0.2\,\mathrm{m}
$$

$$
y = 0.3\,\mathrm{m}
$$

$$
q = 2 \times 10^{-6}\,\mathrm{C}
$$

First compute:

$$
a^2 + y^2
=
0.2^2 + 0.3^2
$$

$$
=
0.04 + 0.09
$$

$$
=
0.13
$$

Now compute

$$
(0.13)^{3/2}
$$

$$
=
0.13 \sqrt{0.13}
$$

$$
\approx 0.13 \times 0.3606
$$

$$
\approx 0.0469
$$

---

### 5. Calculate $E_x$

$$
E_x =
-
9 \times 10^9
\cdot
2 \times 10^{-6}
\cdot
\frac{0.2}{0.0469}
$$

First:

$$
9 \times 10^9 \times 2 \times 10^{-6}
=
18000
$$

Then:

$$
18000 \times 0.2 = 3600
$$

Finally:

$$
E_x = -\frac{3600}{0.0469}
$$

$$
E_x \approx -7.68 \times 10^4\,\mathrm{N/C}
$$

---

### 6. Calculate $E_y$

$$
E_y =
3
\cdot
9 \times 10^9
\cdot
2 \times 10^{-6}
\cdot
\frac{0.3}{0.0469}
$$

First:

$$
3 \times 9 \times 10^9 \times 2 \times 10^{-6}
=
54000
$$

Then:

$$
54000 \times 0.3 = 16200
$$

Thus:

$$
E_y = \frac{16200}{0.0469}
$$

$$
E_y \approx 3.45 \times 10^5\,\mathrm{N/C}
$$

---

### 7. Field Magnitude

$$
|\vec E| =
\sqrt{E_x^2 + E_y^2}
$$

$$
|\vec E|
=
\sqrt{
(7.68 \times 10^4)^2
+
(3.45 \times 10^5)^2
}
$$

$$
|\vec E|
\approx 3.53 \times 10^5\,\mathrm{N/C}
$$

---

### 8. Limit for $y \gg a$

If

$$
y \gg a
$$

then

$$
a^2 + y^2 \approx y^2
$$

Thus,

$$
E_x \approx -kq \frac{a}{y^3}
$$

and

$$
E_y \approx 3kq \frac{1}{y^2}
$$

The vertical component dominates for large distances.

---

### 9. Zero-Field Point

For a zero-field point:

$$
E_x = 0
$$

and

$$
E_y = 0
$$

On the $y$-axis:

$$
E_y =
3kq
\frac{y}{(a^2+y^2)^{3/2}}
$$

This is zero only when

$$
y = 0
$$

But at

$$
y=0
$$

the horizontal component remains nonzero because the charges are unequal.

Therefore:

$$
\vec E \neq 0
$$

on the $y$-axis.

---

## Final Result

$$
E_x \approx -7.68 \times 10^4\,\mathrm{N/C}
$$

$$
E_y \approx 3.45 \times 10^5\,\mathrm{N/C}
$$

$$
|\vec E| \approx 3.53 \times 10^5\,\mathrm{N/C}
$$

No zero-field point exists on the $y$-axis.

---

## Interpretation

The stronger charge $+2q$ dominates the field configuration.

Far from the charges, the system behaves approximately like a single point charge with total charge:

$$
3q
$$

located near the origin.

# Task 4 – Motion of a Particle in a Uniform Field

## Problem Statement

A charged particle moves in a uniform electric field.

Given:

$$
m = 0.02\,\mathrm{kg}
$$

$$
q = 1\,\mathrm{mC}
$$

$$
\vec E = (30,100)\,\mathrm{N/C}
$$

$$
\vec v(0) = (20,0)\,\mathrm{m/s}
$$

$$
\vec r(0) = (0,0)
$$

Determine:

1. Equations of motion
2. The trajectory
3. The time required to reach

$$
v_y = 50\,\mathrm{m/s}
$$

4. The kinetic energy at

$$
t = 0.05\,\mathrm{s}
$$

5. Consistency with energy conservation

---

## Theory

The electric force is

$$
\vec F = q \vec E
$$

Newton’s second law gives

$$
m \vec a = q \vec E
$$

Thus,

$$
\vec a = \frac{q}{m}\vec E
$$

For constant acceleration:

$$
\vec v(t) = \vec v_0 + \vec a t
$$

$$
\vec r(t) = \vec r_0 + \vec v_0 t + \frac{1}{2}\vec a t^2
$$

---

## Step-by-Step Solution

### 1. Convert Units

$$
q = 1\,\mathrm{mC} = 10^{-3}\,\mathrm{C}
$$

---

### 2. Calculate Acceleration

The acceleration components are

$$
a_x =
\frac{qE_x}{m}
$$

$$
a_y =
\frac{qE_y}{m}
$$

Substitute values:

$$
a_x =
\frac{10^{-3} \cdot 30}{0.02}
$$

$$
a_x = \frac{0.03}{0.02}
$$

$$
a_x = 1.5\,\mathrm{m/s^2}
$$

Similarly:

$$
a_y =
\frac{10^{-3} \cdot 100}{0.02}
$$

$$
a_y = \frac{0.1}{0.02}
$$

$$
a_y = 5\,\mathrm{m/s^2}
$$

Thus:

$$
\vec a = (1.5,5)\,\mathrm{m/s^2}
$$

---

### 3. Velocity Equations

Initial velocity:

$$
\vec v_0 = (20,0)
$$

Therefore,

$$
v_x(t) = 20 + 1.5t
$$

$$
v_y(t) = 5t
$$

---

### 4. Position Equations

Using

$$
x(t) = x_0 + v_{0x}t + \frac{1}{2}a_x t^2
$$

$$
x(t) =
20t + 0.75 t^2
$$

Similarly:

$$
y(t) =
\frac{1}{2}(5)t^2
$$

$$
y(t) = 2.5 t^2
$$

---

### 5. Time for $v_y = 50\,\mathrm{m/s}$

We use

$$
v_y = 5t
$$

Set

$$
v_y = 50
$$

Thus,

$$
50 = 5t
$$

$$
t = 10\,\mathrm{s}
$$

---

### 6. Velocity at $t = 0.05\,\mathrm{s}$

Horizontal velocity:

$$
v_x = 20 + 1.5(0.05)
$$

$$
v_x = 20.075\,\mathrm{m/s}
$$

Vertical velocity:

$$
v_y = 5(0.05)
$$

$$
v_y = 0.25\,\mathrm{m/s}
$$

Total speed:

$$
v =
\sqrt{20.075^2 + 0.25^2}
$$

$$
v \approx 20.08\,\mathrm{m/s}
$$

---

### 7. Kinetic Energy

$$
K = \frac{1}{2}mv^2
$$

Substitute values:

$$
K =
\frac{1}{2}
\cdot
0.02
\cdot
(20.08)^2
$$

$$
K = 0.01 \times 403.2
$$

$$
K \approx 4.03\,\mathrm{J}
$$

---

## Final Result

$$
\vec a = (1.5,5)\,\mathrm{m/s^2}
$$

$$
v_x(t) = 20 + 1.5t
$$

$$
v_y(t) = 5t
$$

$$
x(t) = 20t + 0.75t^2
$$

$$
y(t) = 2.5t^2
$$

$$
t(v_y=50) = 10\,\mathrm{s}
$$

$$
K(0.05\,\mathrm{s}) \approx 4.03\,\mathrm{J}
$$

---

## Interpretation

The motion is analogous to projectile motion under gravity, except that the acceleration is generated by the electric field.

The particle continuously gains kinetic energy because the electric field performs work on it.

# Task 5 – Capacitor: Energy and Force

## Problem Statement

A parallel-plate capacitor is given with:

$$
S = 0.02\,\mathrm{m^2}
$$

$$
d = 5\,\mathrm{mm}
$$

$$
U = 500\,\mathrm{V}
$$

Determine:

1. The capacitance
2. The stored energy
3. The electric field intensity
4. The energy density
5. The force between the plates

---

## Theory

For a parallel-plate capacitor:

$$
C = \varepsilon_0 \frac{S}{d}
$$

where

$$
\varepsilon_0 = 8.85 \times 10^{-12}\,\mathrm{F/m}
$$

Stored energy:

$$
W = \frac{1}{2}CU^2
$$

Electric field:

$$
E = \frac{U}{d}
$$

Energy density:

$$
u = \frac{1}{2}\varepsilon_0 E^2
$$

Force between plates:

$$
F = \frac{1}{2}\varepsilon_0 E^2 S
$$

---

## Step-by-Step Solution

### 1. Convert Units

Distance:

$$
d = 5\,\mathrm{mm} = 5 \times 10^{-3}\,\mathrm{m}
$$

---

### 2. Capacitance

Use:

$$
C = \varepsilon_0 \frac{S}{d}
$$

Substitute values:

$$
C =
8.85 \times 10^{-12}
\cdot
\frac{0.02}{5 \times 10^{-3}}
$$

Compute:

$$
\frac{0.02}{0.005} = 4
$$

Thus:

$$
C = 8.85 \times 10^{-12} \times 4
$$

$$
C = 3.54 \times 10^{-11}\,\mathrm{F}
$$

---

### 3. Stored Energy

$$
W = \frac{1}{2}CU^2
$$

Substitute values:

$$
W =
\frac{1}{2}
\cdot
3.54 \times 10^{-11}
\cdot
(500)^2
$$

Compute:

$$
500^2 = 250000
$$

Thus:

$$
W =
0.5
\cdot
3.54 \times 10^{-11}
\cdot
250000
$$

$$
W =
4.425 \times 10^{-6}\,\mathrm{J}
$$

---

### 4. Electric Field Intensity

$$
E = \frac{U}{d}
$$

Substitute:

$$
E =
\frac{500}{5 \times 10^{-3}}
$$

$$
E = 100000\,\mathrm{V/m}
$$

Therefore,

$$
E = 1 \times 10^5\,\mathrm{V/m}
$$

---

### 5. Energy Density

$$
u = \frac{1}{2}\varepsilon_0 E^2
$$

Substitute:

$$
u =
\frac{1}{2}
\cdot
8.85 \times 10^{-12}
\cdot
(10^5)^2
$$

Compute:

$$
(10^5)^2 = 10^{10}
$$

Thus:

$$
u =
0.5
\cdot
8.85 \times 10^{-12}
\cdot
10^{10}
$$

$$
u = 4.425 \times 10^{-2}\,\mathrm{J/m^3}
$$

---

### 6. Force Between Plates

$$
F = \frac{1}{2}\varepsilon_0 E^2 S
$$

Substitute values:

$$
F =
0.5
\cdot
8.85 \times 10^{-12}
\cdot
10^{10}
\cdot
0.02
$$

$$
F = 8.85 \times 10^{-4}\,\mathrm{N}
$$

---

## Final Result

$$
C = 3.54 \times 10^{-11}\,\mathrm{F}
$$

$$
W = 4.43 \times 10^{-6}\,\mathrm{J}
$$

$$
E = 1 \times 10^5\,\mathrm{V/m}
$$

$$
u = 4.43 \times 10^{-2}\,\mathrm{J/m^3}
$$

$$
F = 8.85 \times 10^{-4}\,\mathrm{N}
$$

---

## Interpretation

The capacitor stores energy in the electric field between its plates.

The attractive force results from the tendency of the electric field to reduce its stored energy by decreasing plate separation.

# Task 6 – 2D Field Map

## Problem Statement

Consider three arbitrary point charges in a plane.

Determine:

1. The electric field function
2. A vector field representation
3. Equilibrium points
4. Stability of equilibrium
5. Comparison with the two-charge case

---

## Theory

The electric field generated by multiple charges is obtained using superposition:

$$
\vec E(x,y)
=
\sum_i
k
\frac{q_i \vec r_i}{|\vec r_i|^3}
$$

where

$$
\vec r_i = (x-x_i,y-y_i)
$$

---

## Step-by-Step Solution

### 1. Example Charge Configuration

Assume:

$$
q_1 = 2\,\mu\mathrm{C}
\quad \text{at} \quad (-0.2,0)
$$

$$
q_2 = -3\,\mu\mathrm{C}
\quad \text{at} \quad (0.2,0)
$$

$$
q_3 = 1\,\mu\mathrm{C}
\quad \text{at} \quad (0,0.3)
$$

---

### 2. Field Function

The total field is

$$
\vec E =
\vec E_1 + \vec E_2 + \vec E_3
$$

with

$$
\vec E_i =
k
\frac{q_i(x-x_i,y-y_i)}
{
[(x-x_i)^2+(y-y_i)^2]^{3/2}
}
$$

---

### 3. Equilibrium Point

An equilibrium point satisfies

$$
\vec E = 0
$$

Thus:

$$
E_x = 0
$$

and

$$
E_y = 0
$$

This system generally requires numerical methods such as Newton iteration.

---

### 4. Stability

Small displacements around equilibrium determine stability.

If nearby field vectors point back toward equilibrium, the point is stable.

If nearby vectors point away, the point is unstable.

Electrostatic equilibria are generally unstable according to Earnshaw’s theorem.

---

### 5. Comparison with Two Charges

For two charges:

- Field geometry is symmetric
- Equilibrium points may occur on the axis connecting charges

For three charges:

- Field topology becomes more complex
- Multiple equilibrium regions may appear

---

## Final Result

General field expression:

$$
\vec E(x,y)
=
\sum_i
k
\frac{q_i(x-x_i,y-y_i)}
{
[(x-x_i)^2+(y-y_i)^2]^{3/2}
}
$$

Equilibrium points satisfy:

$$
E_x = E_y = 0
$$

---

## Interpretation

Field maps visualize how electric forces guide charged particles.

Three-charge systems generate much richer field geometries than two-charge systems.

# Task 7 – Motion in a Central Field

## Problem Statement

Consider the electric field:

$$
E(r) = k \frac{Q}{r^2}\hat r
$$

Determine:

1. The equation of motion
2. The radial-motion case
3. RK4 numerical implementation concept
4. Positive and negative energy cases
5. Comparison with gravitation

---

## Theory

The electric force acting on charge $q$ is

$$
\vec F =
q\vec E
$$

Thus:

$$
m\ddot r =
k\frac{qQ}{r^2}
$$

---

## Step-by-Step Solution

### 1. Equation of Motion

Using Newton’s law:

$$
m\frac{d^2r}{dt^2}
=
k\frac{qQ}{r^2}
$$

Therefore:

$$
\ddot r =
\frac{kqQ}{mr^2}
$$

---

### 2. Radial Motion

If motion occurs only along the radial direction:

$$
\theta = \text{constant}
$$

Then the problem becomes one-dimensional.

For opposite charges:

$$
qQ < 0
$$

the force is attractive.

For equal signs:

$$
qQ > 0
$$

the force is repulsive.

---

### 3. RK4 Numerical Method

Define:

$$
v = \frac{dr}{dt}
$$

Then:

$$
\frac{dv}{dt}
=
\frac{kqQ}{mr^2}
$$

The RK4 method updates:

$$
r_{n+1}
$$

and

$$
v_{n+1}
$$

using weighted averages of intermediate slopes.

---

### 4. Total Energy

The total mechanical energy is

$$
E =
\frac{1}{2}mv^2
+
k\frac{qQ}{r}
$$

If:

$$
E < 0
$$

the motion is bounded.

If:

$$
E > 0
$$

the particle escapes to infinity.

---

### 5. Gravitational Analogy

Electrostatic and gravitational forces both obey inverse-square laws:

$$
F \propto \frac{1}{r^2}
$$

The equations therefore have identical mathematical structure.

---

## Final Result

Equation of motion:

$$
m\ddot r =
k\frac{qQ}{r^2}
$$

Energy:

$$
E =
\frac{1}{2}mv^2
+
k\frac{qQ}{r}
$$

---

## Interpretation

Central-force motion explains orbital trajectories and scattering processes.

The electric case differs from gravity because electrostatic forces may be either attractive or repulsive.

# Task 8 – Energy of a Three-Charge System

2. The energy for an equilateral triangle
3. The effect of changing one charge sign
4. The minimum-energy configuration
5. Stability interpretation

---

## Theory

The total electrostatic potential energy is the sum over all charge pairs:

$$
U =
k
\sum_{i<j}
\frac{q_i q_j}{r_{ij}}
$$

---

## Step-by-Step Solution

### 1. Three Charges

Assume:

$$
q_1 = q_2 = q_3 = q
$$

located at the vertices of an equilateral triangle of side length:

$$
a
$$

---

### 2. Pair Energies

There are three interacting pairs:

$$
(q_1,q_2)
$$

$$
(q_1,q_3)
$$

$$
(q_2,q_3)
$$

Each contributes:

$$
k\frac{q^2}{a}
$$

Thus:

$$
U =
3k\frac{q^2}{a}
$$

---

### 3. Example Calculation

Take:

$$
q = 2 \times 10^{-6}\,\mathrm{C}
$$

$$
a = 0.3\,\mathrm{m}
$$

Substitute:

$$
U =
3
\cdot
9 \times 10^9
\cdot
\frac{(2 \times 10^{-6})^2}{0.3}
$$

Compute:

$$
(2 \times 10^{-6})^2
=
4 \times 10^{-12}
$$

Thus:

$$
U =
27 \times 10^9
\cdot
\frac{4 \times 10^{-12}}{0.3}
$$

$$
U =
0.36\,\mathrm{J}
$$

---

### 4. Changing One Charge Sign

If one charge becomes negative:

$$
q_3 \to -q
$$

then two pair energies become negative.

The total energy decreases, producing a more stable configuration.

---

### 5. Minimum-Energy Configuration

Minimum energy occurs when opposite charges are positioned close together.

Like charges prefer larger separation distances.

---

## Final Result

For equal charges:

$$
U =
3k\frac{q^2}{a}
$$

Numerically:

$$
U = 0.36\,\mathrm{J}
$$

---

The sign of the energy determines whether the configuration is energetically favorable.

Negative-energy systems are generally more stable.

# Task 9 – Dipole in an External Field

## Problem Statement

A dipole is placed in a uniform electric field.

Determine:

1. The torque
2. The potential energy
3. The angular equation of motion
4. The small-angle approximation
5. Harmonic-oscillator interpretation

---

## Theory

A dipole consists of charges:

$$
+q
\quad \text{and} \quad
-q
$$

separated by distance:

$$
d
$$

The dipole moment is

$$
\vec p = q\vec d
$$

---

## Step-by-Step Solution

### 1. Torque on the Dipole

The torque magnitude is

$$
\tau = pE\sin\theta
$$

where:

- $p$ is the dipole moment
- $E$ is the external field
- $\theta$ is the angle between $\vec p$ and $\vec E$

The torque tends to align the dipole with the field.

---

### 2. Potential Energy

The potential energy is

$$
U = -pE\cos\theta
$$

Minimum energy occurs when:

$$
\theta = 0
$$

---

### 3. Angular Equation of Motion

Using rotational dynamics:

$$
I\ddot\theta = -pE\sin\theta
$$

where:

$$
I
$$

is the moment of inertia.

---

### 4. Small-Angle Approximation

For small angles:

$$
\sin\theta \approx \theta
$$

Thus:

$$
I\ddot\theta = -pE\theta
$$

or

$$
\ddot\theta +
\frac{pE}{I}\theta = 0
$$

---

### 5. Harmonic Oscillator Form

This is identical to the harmonic oscillator equation:

$$
\ddot x + \omega^2 x = 0
$$

with angular frequency:

$$
\omega =
\sqrt{\frac{pE}{I}}
$$

---

## Final Result

Torque:

$$
\tau = pE\sin\theta
$$

Potential energy:

$$
U = -pE\cos\theta
$$

Angular equation:

$$
I\ddot\theta = -pE\sin\theta
$$

Small oscillation frequency:

$$
\omega =
\sqrt{\frac{pE}{I}}
$$

---

## Interpretation

A dipole in an electric field behaves similarly to a pendulum.

Small angular oscillations produce harmonic motion around equilibrium.

# Task 10 – Electric Flux and Gauss’s Law

## Problem Statement

Determine:

1. The electric flux definition
2. Flux through a sphere
3. Discrete approximation
4. Dependence on grid resolution
5. Comparison with the analytical result

---

## Theory

Electric flux is defined as

$$
\Phi =
\oint
\vec E \cdot d\vec A
$$

Gauss’s law states:

$$
\Phi =
\frac{q_{\mathrm{enc}}}{\varepsilon_0}
$$

---

## Step-by-Step Solution

### 1. Field of a Point Charge

For a point charge:

$$
E =
k\frac{q}{r^2}
$$

The field is radial.

---

### 2. Sphere of Radius $R$

The sphere surface area is

$$
A = 4\pi R^2
$$

Since the field magnitude is constant on the sphere:

$$
\Phi = EA
$$

Substitute:

$$
\Phi =
\left(
k\frac{q}{R^2}
\right)
(4\pi R^2)
$$

Cancel $R^2$:

$$
\Phi = 4\pi k q
$$

Since

$$
k = \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0}
$$

we obtain

$$
\Phi =
\frac{q}{\varepsilon_0}
$$

---

### 3. Numerical Example

Take:

$$
q = 1 \times 10^{-6}\,\mathrm{C}
$$

Then:

$$
\Phi =
\frac{10^{-6}}{8.85 \times 10^{-12}}
$$

$$
\Phi \approx 1.13 \times 10^5\,\mathrm{N\,m^2/C}
$$

---

### 4. Discrete Approximation

The sphere surface is divided into small patches:

$$
\Delta A_i
$$

The flux approximation is

$$
\Phi \approx
\sum_i
\vec E_i \cdot \Delta \vec A_i
$$

As the number of grid points increases:

$$
N \to \infty
$$

the numerical result converges to the analytical value.

---

### 5. Comparison

Analytical result:

$$
\Phi =
\frac{q}{\varepsilon_0}
$$

Numerical approximations approach the same value with increasing resolution.

---

## Final Result

$$
\Phi =
\frac{q}{\varepsilon_0}
$$

For:

$$
q = 10^{-6}\,\mathrm{C}
$$

the flux is

$$
\Phi \approx 1.13 \times 10^5\,\mathrm{N\,m^2/C}
$$

---

## Interpretation

Gauss’s law demonstrates that electric flux depends only on enclosed charge, not on the shape of the surface.

This principle is fundamental for solving highly symmetric electrostatic problems.
